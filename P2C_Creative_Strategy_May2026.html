A note before we begin

This is a draft, an offering, a place to push from.

Power to Change has 50+ years of grassroots fruit, a clear mission, and a faithful team. Nothing in this document changes that. What follows is an attempt to give that fruit a clearer voice — one that sounds like us, that a pastor in Halifax and a student in Vancouver can both repeat without translating, and that holds together across nine ministries without flattening any of them.

This creative services strategy aims to use a voice it recommends: a voice humble enough to admit where things break, convicted enough to say what we believe, warm enough to be human, plain enough to be remembered. The strategy is worth pursuing only if we are willing to be changed by it.

If something here is wrong, we want to find out. If something here is right, we want to make it real.

"Until all know Jesus." — Power to Change

01 / The Diagnosis

Where clarity breaks.

Power to Change is not a brand problem in search of a brand solution. It is a clarity problem. The mission is clear. The fruit is real. What slips, when we say who we are, is the relationship between the whole and the parts — and the words we use to describe it.

Four breaks recur. None of them are fatal. All of them are fixable.

Break 1 — The umbrella metaphor

"Umbrella" is the word that gets reached for when staff or partners describe how P2C and its ministries fit together. It is a passive word. An umbrella shelters things that exist independently. It implies that Athletes in Action, FamilyLife Canada, DRIME, and the rest are separate entities that happen to share a roof. That is not what is true. P2C and its ministries are one mission expressed in different rooms — not nine organizations standing under one cover.

Break 2 — Internal language used externally

"Hub of Evangelism." "10,000 Communities on Mission." "The four C's." "20-mile march." These are excellent internal frames for staff and the board. They are not the right shape for a churchgoer in Saskatoon or a 22-year-old who has just decided to follow Jesus. Right now, our internal language and our external language are the same language. The strategy is not to abandon the internal frames — it is to translate them, deliberately, into language a stranger could use.

Break 3 — Visual disconnection across ministries

Each P2C ministry has its own logo, palette, and visual personality. The break is that nothing in the visual system signals that they belong to one another. A donor looking at FamilyLife Canada and Athletes in Action side by side has no visual reason to believe they are part of the same mission. The connective tissue is in our org chart, not on the page.

Break 4 — A name that is two names

"Power to Change" and "P2C" coexist across our materials with no clear rule about which to use when. Externally, "P2C" requires translation that "Power to Change" does not. We need a simple convention so we stop teaching every audience our shorthand.

What this costs us: Donors and partners who can't describe us to a friend. Ministry teams who default to their own brand because the parent feels abstract. A new generation of Christ-followers who hear nine separate pitches when they could be invited to join one movement. And — quietly — a sense among our own staff that "P2C" is more administrative than missional, when in fact it is the mission.

02 / The Strategic Idea

One mission, many expressions.

This is the central frame this entire document is built on. Everything that follows — the one-word answer, the one-sentence answer, the website direction, the logo system, the voice — is a translation of this single idea.

1 One mission

Helping people know Jesus and experience His power to change the world.

9 Nine ministries

Each a distinct expression of that mission, shaped for a different sphere — campuses, families, athletes, leaders, the vulnerable, the digital world, the streets, the marketplace, the world.

1 One movement

Nine expressions, one movement — Power to Change.

This frame replaces "umbrella" with "movement." An umbrella covers. A movement carries. People belong to movements; they only stand under umbrellas.

"We are one mission with nine front doors."

03 / Identity

Who and what is Power to Change?

Three answers, each shorter than the last. Use the one the moment calls for.

The one word.

Movement.

"Movement" does work that "organization," "network," "umbrella," and "ministry" cannot. It says we are going somewhere. It says people, not departments. It says we expect to grow, and we expect to be joined. Use it as a noun: "Power to Change is a movement." Use it as a posture: "we move with the Spirit, with the church, and with one another."

The one sentence.

Power to Change is a Canadian movement of nine ministries, helping people know Jesus and experience His power to change the world.

This is the version a person could say at a dinner table. Twenty-three words. It contains everything: where we are (Canadian), what we are (a movement), how we are organized (nine ministries), and why we exist.

The one paragraph.

Power to Change is a Canadian movement of nine ministries with one shared mission: helping people know Jesus and experience His power to change the world. For 50+ years, our staff and volunteers have shared the gospel, made disciples, and equipped Christ-followers to live on mission across campuses, families, sport, the marketplace, the digital world, and beyond. Today, we are working to ignite ten thousand communities on mission across Canada and the world — until all know Jesus.

05 / Voice and Tone

Humble and convicted.

Two words, held together. Drop either one and our voice goes wrong: humble without conviction sounds embarrassed about the gospel; convicted without humility sounds like a sermon at someone. Most Christian organizations default to one or the other. P2C's opportunity is to refuse the trade.

Trait What it sounds like What it doesn't
Humble We do not have it all figured out. We learn from the church and seekers. Self-deprecating, apologetic for the gospel, hedging.
Convicted Jesus is real, He changes lives, we have seen it, we are not embarrassed to say so. Loud, certain about everything, scolding.
Warm First-person, simple, kind, authentic. Sentences a friend would say. Corporate, distant, polished to the point of soulless.
Plain Short words when short words will do. One idea per sentence. Jargon, theological insider terms unexplained, marketing speak.
Active We are doing things. People are doing things. The Spirit is doing things. Passive voice, abstract nouns, hedging verbs.

The voice test

Before something goes out, read it aloud and ask:

  • Could a person who is not yet a Christian read this without flinching at the tone?
  • Could a pastor read this without finding it shallow?
  • Could a P2C staff member read this and feel it sounds like us?

If any answer is no, rewrite.

Two short examples

Off-voice
P2C is a leading evangelical organization committed to leveraging strategic innovation to maximize Kingdom impact across Canadian society.
On-voice
Power to Change is a Canadian movement of nine ministries, helping people know Jesus. We have been at this for fifty years. We are still learning.
Off-voice
Through our Hub of Evangelism, we are catalysing 10,000 Communities on Mission across diverse cultural spheres.
On-voice
We are praying for ten thousand communities across Canada — small groups, campuses, teams, neighbourhoods — where people are finding Jesus and helping others find Him too.
Off-voice
Join us in fulfilling the Great Commission through our turnkey resources.
On-voice
We would love your help. Pray for us. Give if you can. Share Jesus where you already are — that is the whole point.
06 / Ministry Relationship

How nine become one — and stay nine.

Each P2C ministry has earned its identity. The strategy is not to flatten the ministries into one visual or verbal style. The strategy is to make their belonging legible.

The naming convention

The lockup — always:

[Ministry name], a Power to Change ministry.

FamilyLife Canada, a Power to Change ministry.  ·  DRIME, a Power to Change ministry.  ·  P2C-Students, a Power to Change ministry.

Change the words we use

Instead of SayingStart saying
FamilyLife Canada is part of P2C's umbrella of ministries.FamilyLife Canada is a Power to Change ministry.
DRIME is one of our nine sub-brands.DRIME is one of nine ministries that make up Power to Change.
P2C-Students operates under the P2C parent organization.P2C-Students helps students take their next step toward Jesus, as part of the Power to Change movement.
Athletes in Action is an independent ministry within P2C.Athletes in Action is a Power to Change ministry — one of nine ways we help people know Jesus.
07 / Visual Identity

Ministries are unique, yet belong.

Three moves, in order of importance: a logo endorsement system, a tightened colour and type palette, and an image direction. None of this requires a full rebrand of any ministry. It requires a shared signature — small, consistent, present everywhere.

Logo endorsement system

Each ministry keeps its primary mark. Beneath it sits a small, consistent Power to Change endorsement. The endorsement is small on purpose — it does not compete with the ministry mark. It just makes belonging visible.

Power to Change — Parent mark

Ministry marks with P2C endorsement lockup

a Power to Change ministry
a Power to Change ministry
a Power to Change ministry

These are illustrative logo concepts designed to encourage brainstorming of ideas. Each ministry uses its official brand colours. The logos are consistent in sizing and use simple concepts of people in different spheres of influence. The P2C cross+p2c mark with a Power to Change Ministry to the right — serves as the consistent endorsement signature across all nine ministries.

Endorsement rule

Primary: each ministry's unique logo, simply redefined to convey it's target reach.
Endorsement: the P2C cross+p2c mark + a Power to Change ministry in a single weight, single colour, set at roughly one-third the height of the primary mark.
Position: directly beneath the primary mark, separated by a thin rule. Or, in horizontal lockups, to the right with a vertical rule between them.
No exceptions for "special occasions." Clear space and minimum size are defined once, in a single brand kit, used by every ministry.

Typography

Montserrat — headings and titles. Clean, contemporary, wide weight range. Available via Google Fonts.

Source Serif Pro — body and paragraphs. Warm, readable, trusted. Pairs with Montserrat to keep us from feeling like a tech company.

Imagery direction

Use images that are
Real people in real moments. Documentary, not catalogue. Visibly Canadian and visibly diverse. Active — people praying, talking, coaching, eating together. Hands and faces. Natural light, soft contrast. The image should feel found, not produced.
Avoid images that are
Lone figures looking at sunsets. Cupped hands holding light. Doves. Generic crosses on hilltops. Stock-image diversity. Heavy filters that flatten skin tones. Conference photography as ministry photography.
08 / Website Direction

p2c.com — what to say first.

The home page has roughly four seconds of patience. Use them on the one sentence, the tagline, and one image that proves the words. Everything else can come after.

Above the fold — hero copy

Helping people know Jesus.Power to Change is a Canadian movement of nine ministries with one shared mission: helping people know Jesus and experience His power to change the world.
Until all know Jesus.

Page structure — in order

  1. Hero — the one sentence + tagline.
  2. "Nine ministries, one mission." — a clean grid of the nine ministry marks, each with one-line description.
  3. "What we believe is happening in Canada right now." — a short, honest read of the spiritual moment in plain language.
  4. "Where it is going." — the 10,000 communities vision, translated externally.
  5. Stories — three or four real moments from across the ministries with ministry photography.
  6. Then the numbers — 455,390 heard the gospel, 31,043 made commitments. Numbers come after stories, never before.
  7. How to take part — Pray. Give. Serve.
  8. Footer — mission, nine ministries listed alphabetically, contact, tagline.

What to remove or quiet

Replace generic stock imagery. Every "click here" and "learn more" should become the actual thing offered: "Read her story," "See the ministries," "Give monthly."

09 / Translating the Big Ideas

Hub of Evangelism, simply understood.

The phrase is doing important work internally. It does not survive the journey to a public audience without translation. Three lengths, for three contexts:

Short — general public
We help the Church share Jesus.
Medium — partners and pastors
Power to Change is becoming a place where Christ-followers, churches, and ministries come together to learn, share what works, and try new things — so more people can hear about Jesus.
Long — strategy page and donor decks
Over fifty years of grassroots evangelism, we have learned what helps people meet Jesus and what does not. Now we are turning that experience outward — building a hub where churches and a new generation of Christ-followers can find tools, training, coaching, and partners for sharing the gospel.

10,000 communities on mission, translated.

For the home page
Ten thousand communities across Canada and beyond — small groups, campuses, teams, neighbourhoods — where people are finding Jesus and helping others find Him too.
For a board deck or grant letter
Within five years, we are praying that ten thousand distinct communities across Canada and the world will be sharing the gospel where they already live and work. Some of these will be P2C-led. Most will not. That is the point.
For a thirty-second answer
Ten thousand groups of Christians — anywhere in Canada — who are sharing Jesus where they already are. That is what we are working toward.
10 / Hard Truths

Honest places to embrace and review.

What we are underestimating
  • The cost of brand fragmentation. Nine ministries with nine voices is more expensive than it looks.
  • How much work "Until all know Jesus" is already doing. We use it as a sign-off when we could use it as a centre of gravity.
  • The next generation's discomfort with the word "evangelism." Lead with stories of changed lives outside the movement, and let the word settle in after.
  • How much our internal language is leaking externally. We are teaching every new visitor our vocabulary instead of speaking theirs.
What Needs Further Consideration
  • Asking whether "Power to Change" itself is the right name for the moment. The question deserves a real conversation.
  • Whether we are willing to give up credit. The hub strategy succeeds only if the fruit grows on other people's trees.
  • Whether "nine ministries" is permanent. Some will change shape, some will sunset, and new expressions will join.
  • Whether our staff actually share the public version of who we are, or whether they default to the playbook version because it is the only one they have memorized.
11 / If We Got There

What would be true about us.

  • We would be visible to the broader Canadian Church — pastors in mid-sized cities would know the name, not just the ministry that visited their campus once.
  • We would have given up control of the credit. Most of the ten thousand communities would not be ours. We would tell their stories anyway.
  • Our nine ministries would feel less like silos. Staff would speak about "the movement" as easily as they speak about "my ministry."
  • Our internal language would have settled into its proper room. Strategy documents would still use "Hub of Evangelism." Public-facing materials would not.
  • Our funding model would have widened. More monthly partners, more church partnerships, and a less anxious dependence on a small number of large foundations.
  • We would be less precious. Some experiments would have failed publicly and been written about honestly. The voice would feel like ours because we had earned the right to say "we are still learning."
  • We would still sound like Power to Change. Nothing in this strategy is meant to replace what is already true and good. The aim is to make it legible.
12 / Implementation

Three phases, no heroics.

This is meant to be done in evenings and Tuesday afternoons, not in a six-month brand sprint. The point is to start doing the right thing in the right order.

1

Days 1–60

Words first

  • Adopt the one-sentence definition as the default movement description. Replace the lead sentence on the home page.
  • Adopt the lockup convention: "[Ministry], a Power to Change ministry." Update email signatures, web footers, and every one-pager.
  • Build the "how we describe ourselves" one-page card for each of the nine ministries. Distribute to staff.
2

Days 60–120

Visuals next

  • Lock the colour and typography rules into a single one-page brand kit. Distribute to ministry leads.
  • Design the P2C endorsement lockup in horizontal and stacked formats. Add to every ministry logo file in shared drives.
  • Commission a small photo library — one photographer, three or four ministries, one season of work.
  • Update the website hero. Begin replacing stock photos as the new library comes in.
3

Months 4–12

Movement next

  • Roll out the website restructure (sections in order). Test with a small group of partners and pastors before full launch.
  • Build a quarterly story-curation rhythm: one published story per ministry per quarter, told plainly.
  • Run the voice test on every donor letter, video script, and social post for ninety days. Track what slips. Recalibrate.
  • Revisit this document at the one-year mark. Mark what worked, what did not, what changed about the moment. This is a living strategy, not a finished one.

Ultimately, none of this is the work. The work is the gospel. This is the scaffolding around the work, made as honest as we know how to make it.
